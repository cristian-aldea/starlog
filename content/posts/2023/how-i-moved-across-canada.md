I was given the opportunity to move to Vacouver for my new job as a Software Development Engineer at Amazon. I had little experience with travel or moving, and there are of course many things I wish I'd had done differently. After living throught this, I wanted to share my experience and perspective in the hopes that it'll be useful to you.

## Timing

When planning my move, I first tried to decide the day I'd arrive in the city. I was looking for a time that would work best for me and my budjet:

- From my observation, Spring and Fall are usually the ideal seasons to move. Prices for movers and renting are pretty good since business is slow, and the weather is warm enough here in Canada that it wouldn't get in the way.
- I avoided moving during the Summer since it's usually the pretty busy time, especially in August/September with many students on the market for a place to rent.
- I was told that winter would be likely the cheapest time to move, but I had to move within the year, and I didn't want to deal with the harsh weather.

## Transportation

After picking a date, I had to consider what would be the best way to get to Vancovuer. I chose to book a one-way flight, since other methods with driving or the train weren't a good fit. This'll depend on your circumstances, so whether by car, train, bus, or plane, you should consider what kind of transportation is most convenient for you.

## Residence

After booking my flight, I had to figure out the housing situation. I'd recommend dealing with the details of your housing sonner rather than later, since otherwise it could lead to unecessary stress like it did for me. As I asked around I understood that there were two decent approaches when looking for an appartment to rent in another city:

- In-person: You look for a place to stay short-term. You then search for housing in-person.
- Virtual: You look for a place online, and move there directly after doing a virtual tour and signing the contract online

Both have their ups and down:

- With the virtual approach:
  - ✅ everything is setup beforehand, making the actual move a breeze
  - ✅ you'll only need to change residence once
  - ❌ it's risky to rent or buy a home virtually. Make sure to deal with trustworthy property managers or find a place through any relocation assistance you might have. I'd avoid making any deals on sites like Craigslist with people you can't meet in person.
- With the in-person method:
  - ✅ You get to experience the city and visit neighborhoods before picking a long-term place to rent
  - ✅ You can avoid scams much more easily by dealing with people in-person
  - ❌ You'll likely have to move your stuff twice. I'd recommend packing lightly or renting a car

I went with the in-person approach, and renting an place for about a month before finding a place on Craigslist. I enjoyed living in the city before picking a place to stay, but consider using whatever approach seems like the best fit for you.

A few tips related to my specific situation:

- Don't ever send payments to someone without first seeing the home or before signing a tenancy agreement. Places like Craigslist have a great set of guidelines to help keep you safe: <https://www.craigslist.org/about/scams>.
- Also, when signing a lease agreement, it's good to double check that it's the proper form. Check your provincial or state government website. Example for BC: https://www2.gov.bc.ca/gov/content/housing-tenancy/residential-tenancies/forms,
- Finally, take advantage of any relocation resources or assistance you might have to help you look for housing or relieve any financial burden you might have with the move.

## Luggage

I had to consider how much stuff I wanted to bring with me on my one-way trip. I initially packed very light since I was moving around a lot. I then considered using shipping and movers once I had a permanent place to stay.

## Paperwork

Something I didn't consider when moving was all the paperwork. Once I had a new address, I had to update my home address to my employer, government and services that I use. I found it helpful to compile a todo list for this to keep track of places I still needed to update.

I also had to book several appoinments to get a new drivers license and medical card since I changed province.

## Adjusting to the new City

My first weeks in Vancouver were busy and disorienting. A few months in, after I settled in a routine, I was happy to have the time to explore the city, but at the same time I felt a bit lost in this unfamiliar new space.

When I started feeling homesick, I'd remind myself of why I went through the move in the first place, and tried my best to enjoy what the city had to offer.
